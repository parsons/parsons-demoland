Etel Adnan: Voyage, War and Exile (excerpt)

I originated in a part of the world that war has made and unmade since the beginning of this century. My memory as well as my daily life are woven with war. I often wondered how as fragile a system as the human body could bear upheavals become chronic as the ones that the Arab East has known and is still experiencing.

I was the only child of a family which has known war and exile as a consequence of war. My father was a Syrian, a high ranking officer of the Ottoman Empire, and my mother was a Greek from Smyrna, her father being a wood-carver; they met and married during World War I. In 1922, Smyrna burned in a cataclysmic fire, the war was lost, the Empire dismembered, so they came to Lebanon and settled in Beirut, a garden city with a view from all over, on the sea they knew and the most beautiful harbor of the Mediterranean.

They exiled themselves into charm and beauty. Geographically it was a bearable exile for my father, for after all, he was on Arab Land and close to his birthplace, Damascus. But he was living another exile, a terrible one this one: the empire that he knew and served had disappeared; he was living on enemy territory, a country under French occupation. I lived since my childhood with a knowledge which remained inscribed in me: that geographical exile is but the frame for an exile which goes much deeper and against which one can do nothing. As for my mother, she thought of Smyrna day and night, suffering from its absence, and often, I would say too often, I saw her questioning the horizon which encircles Beirut and wondering where Smyrna would have been, behind which cloud, on which side of the North of the setting sun. Her education had been left to hazard and to the meanderings of her life, and she resided in the twilight zone where most people live and in which they orient themselves according to their desires and sorrows.

A little after I turned twenty, I started considering going to Paris for studies in Philosophy. Considering was a term which can hardly describe my state of mind. I was impatient, yearning to go, and I did get there. My father had already died and my mother was left behind by me, with a pain whose scope I realized only much later. The amplitude of her sorrow took years to reach me. I was a bird then who flew out of her cage and was desirous to go further away. Everywhere and nowhere. Going to the café in those years was already a voyage and that is still one of my favorite trips.

But I went beyond the café. After Paris, I went to California by a series of occurrences which I am not going to linger on today. I landed in Berkeley, far, very far, from every point of view, from Beirut and from everything I knew.

This break which proved to be a radical one, was not an exile. It was adventure. I had decided to give myself a destiny for which nothing had prepared me. After a few years of study at U.C. Berkeley, and Harvard, I found a teaching job at a small college near San Francisco, and gave up my preparations for a Ph.D. ( I should say, most happily)! It was in 1958 and I considered that I had found a roof, a salary, a reason to be, some kind of a new identity. Then followed the Sixties and the worldwide cultural revolution that they brought about. I remember then as a period where I became intensely American, and intensely an Arab, given the new perspective I acquired, from the Bay Area, on the Arab World. Most of all I sensed an exhilarating belonging to the world per se. I was not living an exile but a freedom which I found on my own and at my own risks. It was fun!

But we know that the Earth contains under its crust formidable plates which move constantly and in contradictory directions, creating earthquakes, and like herself, I was creating, within my soul, opposite currents equally ominous: I was espousing the dreams of the Arabs, their dramatic dreams of freedom, dignity, and unity, all symbolized then by Gamal Abdel Nasser; in the same time, and in another direction, I was plunging wholeheartedly into America, its history, its literature (falling in lasting love with the American Indians and with jazz music), aware and practically hypnotized by its frightful possibilities of mass destruction and by its incredible conquests of the moon and of space. I was living in a new time and space scale, in the absolute sense of the present, merging my own mythical time with America’s own. Following the Apollo missions and its rockets, I was extra-territorial and tuned to the universe.

But the foreseeable earthquake did manifest itself; war broke out in 1967, involving Egypt, Syria, and Jordan, and America did decisively participate in the destruction of the Arabs, or their dreams and their lands, and I experienced terror, a terror that my surroundings were not sharing, and I started losing ground, getting not confused, but wounded, isolated, detached although still attached, a conflict which later made me ill for practically a whole year, from 1971 to 1972. I felt the urge to break away, go back to Beirut without looking behind me and not knowing what the future would be. I had come full circle. In the summer of ’72, I was returning to Beirut literally on a stretcher. I had left as a voyager and I was coming back in exile.

I had no family left in Beirut by that summer of ’72. On my father’s side, my half-sisters and brother were living in Damascus. My mother’s own brothers and sister had been dispersed after the fire of Smyrna in different places such as Cyprus, Alexandria, Saloniki and Genova. But it was home, after all, the more so that I was thinking that I was coming back “for good.” I found a tiny apartment with a great view, five stories to climb with steep stairs, and no telephone. I could barely walk then, and I had to rebuild myself, physically and morally, while looking for a job. I reconnected with childhood friends and those I had made during the few trips that I took to Beirut during the regular summer vacations of my teaching years.

A new paper in the French language was being launched and I was hired as the editor of its cultural daily pages. The personnel was made of mostly young women or men whose enthusiasm was contagious. There was excitement in the air and these young people had the impression that they were participating in the creation of a new world, a better one, where Arabs will recover their rights and cease to be excluded from the decisions which concerned them the most.

I was both sharing the feelings of this new generation and expecting the worst. Beirut was a laboratory and a stirring volcano. I had, after all, after Black September of 1970 and after Nasser’s death, written a long poem on Beirut, the “Beirut Hell Express,” which was announcing Beirut’s total destruction. Of course, one does not follow one’s visionary knowledge, but rather gives-in to one’s most obscure wishes and desires.

War did break out again, the civil war which started in April 1975. It was as if a galaxy of fire and death had been born and had started to churn with no end in sight. Like all galaxies it fed on its own energy to its bitter end.

A real exodus followed: first a trickle, then a gradual and constant flow. The Lebanese started to leave. The buildings were disappearing too, the neighborhoods, the streets. With every wall which was falling and every man dying, something of a collective life and memory was being obliterated.

I was discovering—living—exile’s profound meaning. What is exile if not the violent and involuntary loss of all the living symbols of one’s identity? At this point, instead of me leaving, it was Beirut which was leaving me, and, we know it now, forever. Here was an exile which was total, absolute: I was in Beirut and I was witnessing that she was never going to grow normally, to remain the core of what she was. I was seeing (without any exaggeration) the meaning of Paradise lost.

Exile is a dispossession with no recourse. It is always accompanied by a feeling of humiliation: it is the “other,” somebody or something, who takes possession of what you give up, or leave behind. It makes you feel pushed aside, as if you were a material object that is to be displaced.

Over the course of a lifetime that spanned almost a century, Etel Adnan (b. 1925, Beirut; d. 2021, Paris) expressed her prodigious creative and intellectual vision in many forms. In addition to being a visual artist, she was a renowned poet, a prominent journalist, and the author of one of the defining novels of the modern Arab world. Adnan’s biography is notable for its rich convergence of cultural influences. She was born in Lebanon to a Greek mother and Syrian father; grew up speaking French, Arabic, and Greek; and as an adult lived for extended periods in Lebanon, the United States, and France. She began to paint in the late 1950s, while working as a professor of philosophy in Northern California. It was a period when, in protest of France’s colonial rule in Algeria, she renounced writing in French and declared that she would begin “painting in Arabic.” (Guggenheim, 2021)
